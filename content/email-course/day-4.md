# Day 4: How to Spot a Wired Contract (And When to Walk Away)

**Subject: Don't waste time on opportunities you can't win**

Over the past 3 days, you've learned how to find recompetes early, track competitors, and focus on the right NAICS codes. Today's lesson is the most important: **How to identify wired contracts and avoid wasting 100+ hours on proposals you'll never win.**

## The Problem: Bid Discipline

The average proposal costs $15K-$50K in staff time. Small firms can't afford to chase everything.

But most BD teams have poor bid discipline. They see an opportunity that "looks like a fit" and chase it, even when the signs point to an incumbent lock or a wired deal.

Result: 10% win rate, burned-out proposal teams, and missed opportunities where you actually had a shot.

## The Solution: The "Is It Wired?" Framework

Before you invest in a proposal, run through this 5-point checklist. If you see 3+ red flags, walk away.

### Red Flag #1: Incumbent Has Multi-Year Track Record

Check USAspending for the current contract holder. If they've held this contract (or similar work with this program office) for 6+ years across multiple recompetes, they're deeply embedded.

**What to look for:**
- Same incumbent across 2+ contract periods
- Excellent CPAR ratings (check PPIRS if available)
- Incumbent recently won other work with this program office

**Example:** A small cybersecurity firm found a $2M Air Force cyber support RFP. Incumbent had held the contract for 9 years across 3 recompetes. All 3 CPARs were "Exceptional." Walk away — the incumbent has institutional knowledge you can't replicate in 30 days.

### Red Flag #2: Suspiciously Specific Requirements

Read the SOW carefully. If you see requirements that perfectly match one company's unique capability or proprietary tool, it's wired.

**What to look for:**
- "Must have experience with [obscure system only the incumbent uses]"
- "Must have staff with [very specific certification held by <50 people nationwide]"
- "Must have performed work at [this exact military installation]"

**Example:** An RFP required "minimum 5 years supporting the XYZ program at Fort Meade with TS/SCI cleared staff holding GIAC GXPN certification." Only 2 companies in the US met this requirement. One was the incumbent.

### Red Flag #3: Short Timeline on Complex Procurement

If the RFP has a 15-day response window for a $5M technical contract, it's designed to favor the incumbent (who's been drafting their proposal for months).

**What to look for:**
- Response time <21 days for contracts >$1M
- Complex technical requirements with minimal Q&A period
- RFP drops right before federal holidays (limiting your time to respond)

**Example:** A $3.5M DevSecOps RFP dropped on December 18 with a deadline of January 5. Only the incumbent had the bandwidth to respond over the holidays.

### Red Flag #4: No Industry Day or Pre-RFP Engagement

If the program office didn't hold an industry day, RFI, or draft RFP review, they're not looking for new vendors. They're checking the "competition" box for a sole-source justification.

**What to look for:**
- No FedBizOpps/SAM.gov presolicitation notice
- No industry day or sources sought
- Program office unresponsive to outreach attempts

**Example:** A small firm tried to reach the program manager 3 times before the RFP dropped. No response. RFP came out, they submitted questions, all answers were "See SOW." They no-bid and saved 80 hours.

### Red Flag #5: Set-Aside Mismatch

If you're an 8(a) firm and the contract was historically 8(a) but is now full-and-open, the program office wants a specific large prime. If you're a large business and it just became 8(a) set-aside, they have a small business in mind.

**What to look for:**
- Set-aside type changed from previous contract period
- Contract bundled or unbundled in ways that favor specific company types
- NAICS code changed to favor certain firm sizes

**Example:** A contract was 8(a) set-aside for 6 years ($2M/year). Suddenly it's full-and-open at $12M total. The program office wanted to bring in a mid-tier prime who couldn't compete under 8(a) rules.

## The "Walk Away" Decision

If you see **3 or more red flags**, no-bid. Spend that proposal time on opportunities where you have a real shot.

If you see **1-2 red flags**, assess whether you can overcome them:
- Can you partner with the incumbent? (teaming vs. competing)
- Can you offer a significantly better technical solution or price?
- Do you have a relationship with the program office that gives you an edge?

If the answer is no, walk away.

## Your Action Item (20 Minutes)

Pull up the last 3 RFPs you bid on and lost. Run them through the "Is It Wired?" framework.

How many red flags did each have? Would you have no-bid if you'd known then what you know now?

Now apply this to your current pipeline. Pick the top 3 opportunities you're tracking. Score each on the 5-point scale.

If any score 3+, consider walking away. Focus your energy on the opportunities where you can actually win.

## Why This Matters

Bid discipline is the #1 difference between high-performing BD teams and struggling ones.

High performers bid **less** but win **more**. They ruthlessly qualify opportunities and only chase pursuits where they have:
1. A legitimate shot at winning (not wired)
2. A differentiated technical or price solution
3. Relationships with the program office

Struggling teams bid on everything and hope something sticks. Result: 10% win rate, burned-out teams, and a reputation for losing.

Master the "Is It Wired?" framework and you'll save hundreds of hours per year. Use that time to position on opportunities where you can actually win.
