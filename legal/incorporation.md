# Incorporation Guide for GovCon Weekly Intelligence

**Prepared:** March 18, 2026

## Executive Summary

**Recommended Entity:** Delaware C Corporation
**Estimated Timeline:** 1-2 weeks
**Estimated Cost:** $1,500-$3,000 (initial setup)
**Key Benefit:** Standard structure for future fundraising, clear liability protection, and credibility with B2B customers

## Why Incorporate?

### Benefits of Forming a Legal Entity
1. **Liability Protection:** Separates personal assets from business liabilities
2. **Tax Optimization:** Allows business expense deductions and potential tax planning
3. **Credibility:** Shows professionalism to B2B customers and potential investors
4. **Future Fundraising:** Standard structure for raising venture capital if you scale
5. **IP Protection:** Clear ownership of intellectual property (AI models, brand, content)
6. **Contracts:** Easier to sign vendor agreements (Beehiiv, Anthropic, payment processors)

### Risks of NOT Incorporating
- Personal liability for business debts and lawsuits
- Commingling of personal and business finances
- Difficulty raising funding or taking on partners later
- Tax complications and missed deductions
- Challenges selling the business in the future

## Entity Type Comparison

| Feature | Sole Proprietorship | LLC | S Corporation | C Corporation |
|---------|---------------------|-----|---------------|---------------|
| **Liability Protection** | None | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| **Tax Treatment** | Pass-through | Pass-through (default) | Pass-through | Double taxation |
| **Setup Cost** | $0-50 | $500-1,000 | $1,000-2,000 | $1,000-2,000 |
| **Ongoing Compliance** | Minimal | Moderate | High | High |

## Recommendation: Delaware C Corporation

### Why Delaware?
1. **Business-Friendly Law:** Well-established corporate law with predictable court system (Court of Chancery)
2. **Investor Expectation:** VCs and angels expect Delaware C-Corps
3. **Flexibility:** Easy to amend bylaws, issue stock, and manage governance
4. **Privacy:** Shareholder names not in public records
5. **No State Corporate Tax:** If not doing business in Delaware (no physical presence)

### Why C Corporation (vs. LLC or S-Corp)?
1. **Investor-Ready:** VCs and institutional investors only invest in C-Corps
2. **Stock Options:** Easy to issue employee stock options (critical for hiring talent)
3. **Multiple Share Classes:** Can create preferred stock for investors, common for founders
4. **Unlimited Shareholders:** No restrictions on number or type of owners (S-Corp limited to 100 U.S. shareholders)
5. **Global Ownership:** Foreign investors allowed (S-Corp restricted to U.S. citizens/residents)
6. **Credibility:** Signals serious growth intent to partners and customers

### Downsides of C-Corp (and why they're acceptable)
1. **Double Taxation:** Corporate profits taxed at 21% federal, then dividends taxed at individual level
   - **Not a concern early:** You'll reinvest profits into growth, not pay dividends
   - **Later benefit:** Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) exemption can eliminate capital gains tax on exit (Section 1202)

2. **More Compliance:** Annual reports, board meetings, corporate formalities
   - **Worth it:** Standard for any serious business; many tasks can be automated with tools like Clerky or Stripe Atlas

3. **Higher Setup Costs:** ~$1,500-3,000 vs. ~$500 for LLC
   - **Worth it:** One-time cost that avoids expensive restructuring later

## Alternative: Start as LLC, Convert Later

If you want to minimize costs and defer the decision, you could:
1. Form a **Delaware LLC** now ($500-800 setup)
2. Elect **taxation as an S-Corp** (File IRS Form 2553) to save on self-employment taxes
3. Convert to C-Corp later when raising funding (conversion cost: ~$2,000-5,000)

### Pros of LLC-to-C-Corp Path
- Lower initial cost
- Simpler early-stage compliance
- Can still take business deductions and protect liability

### Cons of LLC-to-C-Corp Path
- **Conversion hassle:** Requires legal/tax advice, potential tax consequences
- **Delays fundraising:** Investors may wait for conversion before committing
- **Missed QSBS clock:** QSBS 5-year holding period starts when C-Corp stock is issued, not when LLC is formed
- **Complicates cap table:** Early advisors/contractors may receive LLC units instead of stock options

**Verdict:** Only choose LLC path if you're certain you won't raise VC funding in the next 1-2 years. Given your vision ("Bloomberg Terminal for GovCon"), C-Corp is safer.

## Incorporation Steps (Delaware C-Corp)

### Step 1: Pre-Incorporation (Days 1-2)
**Choose a Company Name**
- Verify availability: [Delaware Division of Corporations](https://icis.corp.delaware.gov/Ecorp/EntitySearch/NameSearch.aspx)
- Reserve name if needed ($75, holds for 120 days)
- Recommended name: **GovCon Intelligence, Inc.** (shorter, more professional than "Weekly Intelligence")

**Select a Registered Agent**
Required in Delaware to receive legal documents. Options:
- **Harvard Business Services:** $50/year, reliable
- **Registered Agents Inc.:** $100/year, includes mail forwarding
- **Corporation Service Company (CSC):** $275/year, premium service

**Founder Structure**
- How many founders? (If solo, you're the sole director and shareholder)
- Stock allocation: If multiple founders, use standard vesting (4-year vest, 1-year cliff)

### Step 2: File Certificate of Incorporation (Day 3)
**Using a Service (Recommended)**
Choose one:
1. **Stripe Atlas** ($500 + state fees)
   - Includes: Incorporation, IRS EIN, 83(b) election, banking intro, legal templates
   - Best for: First-time founders, all-in-one simplicity
   - Timeline: 1 week

2. **Clerky** ($799 + state fees for full package)
   - Includes: Incorporation, stock issuance, advisor/employee agreements, board consents
   - Best for: Founders who want more control and customization
   - Timeline: 1-2 weeks

3. **Lawyer** ($2,000-5,000)
   - Custom documents, personalized advice, investor intros
   - Best for: Complex situations (multiple founders, early investors)
   - Timeline: 2-3 weeks

**DIY Option** (Not Recommended for First-Time Founders)
- File directly with Delaware: $89 filing fee + $50 agent
- Use free templates (risky — errors can cause issues later)
- Still need bylaws, stock issuance, 83(b) election

**What Gets Filed:**
- Certificate of Incorporation (includes company name, stock structure, registered agent)
- Authorized shares: Recommend 10,000,000 shares (standard for startups)
- Par value: $0.0001/share (standard)

### Step 3: Post-Incorporation Setup (Days 4-10)

**Obtain EIN (Employer Identification Number)**
- File IRS Form SS-4 online (free, instant): [IRS EIN Assistant](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)
- Needed for: Bank account, tax filings, hiring employees

**Adopt Bylaws**
Corporate governance rules. Use template from Stripe Atlas/Clerky or lawyer.

**Issue Founder Stock**
- Issue 8,000,000 shares to founder(s) at $0.0001/share ($800 total)
- Leaves 2,000,000 shares for option pool and future investors
- Sign stock purchase agreement and stock certificate

**File 83(b) Election**
**CRITICAL:** Must file within 30 days of stock issuance.
- Elects to pay tax on stock NOW (at low/zero value) rather than later (at higher value)
- Saves significant taxes if company increases in value
- Send to IRS via certified mail, keep proof of filing

**Open Business Bank Account**
Recommended banks:
- **Mercury:** Best for startups, easy online setup, integrates with accounting software
- **Brex:** Good if you need credit card, cashback rewards
- **Chase Business:** Traditional bank, useful for wire transfers and physical checks

Documents needed:
- Certificate of Incorporation
- EIN letter from IRS
- Bylaws
- Founder ID (driver's license/passport)

**Set Up Accounting**
- **QuickBooks Online** ($30/month): Standard for small businesses
- **Xero** ($13-70/month): Good alternative
- **Wave** (Free): Suitable for very early stage

Connect to bank account, categorize expenses, track revenue.

**Register to Do Business in Your State**
If you're operating from a state other than Delaware (e.g., California, New York), you may need to:
- File foreign corporation registration in your home state ($100-800)
- Obtain state tax ID
- Pay annual franchise tax

**Example:** If you're based in California, register as a foreign corporation ($100) and pay $800/year minimum franchise tax.

### Step 4: Ongoing Compliance (Annual)

**Delaware Requirements:**
- **Annual Franchise Tax:** Due March 1 each year
  - Calculation: Based on authorized shares or assumed par value method
  - Cost: Minimum $400/year for C-Corps with 10M authorized shares
- **Annual Report:** Due March 1 ($50 filing fee)

**Federal Requirements:**
- **Tax Return (Form 1120):** Due 15th day of 4th month after fiscal year-end (April 15 for calendar year)
- **Estimated Taxes:** Quarterly (Form 1120-W) if profitable

**State Requirements:**
- Varies by state of operation (income tax, sales tax, employment taxes)

**Corporate Formalities:**
- Hold annual board meeting (document with written consent)
- Keep corporate records (stock ledger, meeting minutes, resolutions)
- Maintain separate business bank account (never commingle personal funds)

## Tax Considerations

### Federal Tax Rate
- C-Corp flat rate: **21%** (Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017)
- Personal income from salary: **10-37%** progressive rates

### Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) - Section 1202
**HUGE tax benefit for founders:**
- If you hold C-Corp stock for **5+ years**, up to **$10 million** (or 10x cost basis) in gains are **tax-free** when you sell
- Requirements:
  - C-Corp with gross assets under $50M
  - Active business (not passive investment)
  - At least 80% of assets used in qualified trade or business
- **Action:** This is why you incorporate as C-Corp NOW, not later — the 5-year clock starts when stock is issued

### Reasonable Compensation
As founder-employee, you must pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (subject to payroll taxes). IRS scrutinizes owner-employees who take low salaries to avoid payroll taxes.

**Strategy early-stage:** You can defer salary while bootstrapping, but document it as "deferred compensation" or "founder sweat equity."

### Tax Deductions
Deductible business expenses:
- Software subscriptions (Beehiiv, Claude API, hosting)
- Home office (if dedicated space, can deduct portion of rent/mortgage)
- Equipment (computer, monitors)
- Marketing and advertising
- Professional services (legal, accounting)
- Travel for business purposes

**Keep records:** Use accounting software and save receipts.

## Founder Stock and Equity

### Initial Stock Issuance
- Issue **8,000,000 shares** at **$0.0001/share** to founder(s)
- Total cost: $800 (pay from personal funds, then reimburse from business account)
- Price: $0.0001/share (par value) — no tax owed since fair market value equals purchase price

### Vesting (if multiple founders)
Even if you trust your co-founder, use vesting:
- **4-year vesting, 1-year cliff**
- If founder leaves before 1 year: gets 0% of stock
- After 1 year: 25% vests
- Remaining 75%: vests monthly over next 3 years

### Option Pool
Reserve **2,000,000 shares** (20% of total) for future employees, advisors, contractors.
- Standard option vesting: 4 years, 1-year cliff
- Early employees: 0.1-1% equity
- Key hires: 1-5% equity
- Advisors: 0.1-0.5% equity

### Cap Table Management
Use cap table software to track ownership:
- **Carta** (industry standard, $1,800/year after free tier)
- **Pulley** ($100-300/month, modern UI)
- **Spreadsheet** (free, but error-prone — use template from Clerky/Atlas)

## Insurance

### Recommended Policies

**General Liability Insurance**
- Covers: Bodily injury, property damage, advertising injury
- Cost: $500-1,500/year
- Recommended: Not critical early (low risk of physical injury for SaaS newsletter)

**Professional Liability Insurance (Errors & Omissions)**
- Covers: Errors in your advice/analysis causing financial harm to customers
- Cost: $1,000-3,000/year
- **RECOMMENDED:** Medium priority — your AI analysis could lead to bad business decisions by customers

**Cyber Liability Insurance**
- Covers: Data breaches, cyberattacks, ransomware
- Cost: $1,000-3,000/year
- **RECOMMENDED:** You collect email addresses and payment info — medium risk

**Directors & Officers (D&O) Insurance**
- Covers: Lawsuits against founders/board members
- Cost: $2,000-5,000/year
- Recommended: Wait until you have investors or significant revenue

**Workers' Compensation Insurance**
- Required if you have employees (not needed for just founders)

### When to Get Insurance
- Phase 1 (Day 1): None required
- Phase 2 (First paying customer): Professional liability
- Phase 3 (100+ subscribers): Add cyber liability
- Phase 4 (Fundraising/employees): Add D&O

## State-Specific Considerations

### If You're Based in California
- Register as foreign corporation: $100
- **Franchise tax:** $800/year minimum (even if no revenue!)
- Income tax: 8.84% on CA-source income
- Sales tax: Not applicable (digital newsletter is not taxable in CA)

### If You're Based in New York
- Register as foreign corporation: $250
- Filing fee: Varies by county
- Income tax: 6.5% (reduced rate for small businesses)

### If You're Based in Texas
- Register as foreign corporation: $750
- No state income tax (major benefit!)
- Franchise tax: 0.375% on revenue above $1.23M (not relevant early)

### If You're Based in Florida
- Register as foreign corporation: $70
- No state income tax
- Annual report: $150

**Strategy:** Consider relocating to a no-income-tax state (TX, FL, NV, WA, TN) if you're remote-flexible.

## Conclusion

**Bottom Line:** Incorporate as a Delaware C Corporation now using Stripe Atlas or Clerky. It costs ~$1,500-3,000 upfront but saves major headaches later and positions you for growth. Don't wait — the QSBS 5-year clock starts when you issue stock, and converting from LLC to C-Corp later is messy and expensive.

**Risk of Waiting:** If you launch as a sole proprietor and gain traction, you'll regret not having liability protection and a clean cap table. Incorporate before you sign your first customer or vendor contract.
